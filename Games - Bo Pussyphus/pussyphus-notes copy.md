# Pussyphus — Working Notes

**Going nowhere in the wrong direction.**

A cat moving UP a downward escalator in a 90s mall.

---

## Core Concept

A browser-based game. A cat on a down escalator in a dead 90s mall. The escalator pulls her down. She goes up anyway. Not in protest — just because that's where she's going. There is no score, no death, no ending. The flow state is the game.

---

## Philosophical Grounding

### Alan Watts — "Nature is wiggly"

Nature is fundamentally curvilinear. Humans impose straight lines, right angles, grids. The straight line is the human imposition. The wiggle is what's actually there. The escalator is the straight line. The mall is the most aggressively rectilinear human space — right angles, floor grids, engineered sightlines. The cat is nature. Wiggly by definition. Moving through the grid with her own responsive, organic logic. Not opposing the straight line — just not organized by it.

The flow state isn't a victory over the mall's geometry. It's what happens when the wiggle and the line coexist without one dominating the other. Watts isn't anti-civilization. The problem is when you mistake the grid for reality instead of recognizing it as an overlay on something that was always wiggly underneath.

### Simone Weil — Attention as suspension of self

Weil defines attention as the suspension of the self — not concentration, not focus, not effort. Attention is what happens when you empty your thought and let it be penetrated by the object. She argues attention so defined is the substance of prayer, the substance of justice, and the substance of learning — all three are the same act.

Willful attention — attention organized around what the self is looking for — destroys the thing it looks at, because it can only find what it already expects. Attention in Weil's sense is receptive, not acquisitive. It doesn't reach for the object. It makes a space the object can enter.

### Heidegger — Ready-to-hand

When you're using a hammer and it's working, your attention goes through the hammer to the nail. The tool disappears. It's transparent. The tool only becomes visible when it breaks — suddenly it's heavy, awkward, present. The broken tool forces self-consciousness.

In flow, the self is ready-to-hand. Functioning, there, but transparent. Attention passes through it toward the world. The cat on the escalator is a self that's ready-to-hand. She doesn't experience herself navigating — she just navigates.

Narcissism is a permanent condition of the self being present-at-hand. Broken in your grip. Always visible, always heavy, always the thing you're attending to even when you think you're attending to something else. You can't look through a cracked lens.

### House of Leaves — Spaces produced by moving through them

The hallway doesn't exist until Navidson measures the wall. The name generates the void. The space is bigger on the inside than on the outside.

Pussyphus is a space that only exists while you're in it, generated by the act of moving through it. The flow state can't be observed from outside, can't be documented without being inside the documentation. The player is the next containment layer out from the cat, just as the reader is the next layer out from Johnny.

### Iris Murdoch — Unselfing

Moral development is not a matter of better choices. It's a development of perception. "Unselfing." Sustained attention that isn't organized around the self's existing categories eventually lets reality revise the picture. Attention is ethics.

### The narcissism connection (structural, not thematic)

The narcissist's failure is a theory-of-mind failure — not at the cognitive level but at the weighting level. They can model what you're thinking but can't grant it ontological standing equal to their own. They can compute your interiority but can't respect it as independent.

The narcissistic failure is exactly what Weil and Murdoch describe as the failure of attention. Not a failure of knowledge — a failure of reception. The self can't suspend itself. Every perception passes through the self's needs first and arrives pre-distorted.

## Two Modes of Self-less Attention

**Released attention:** The self relaxes its grip. Attention flows toward reality as it is. Weil, Murdoch, flow, the cat's wiggle.

**Captured attention:** The self is bypassed. Attention is redirected toward engineered stimuli. The mall, the feed, the dopamine loop.

They look identical from outside. Someone in flow and someone doomscrolling are both "absorbed," both "not self-conscious." The structural difference is total: released attention is responsive to what's actually there; captured attention is responsive to what's been designed to trigger it.

The cat in the mall disambiguates them. Inside the capture architecture but not captured. Not because she's resisting — resistance would require a self braced against something. She's not captured because her attention was never organized around the self in the first place, so there's nothing to hijack.

---

## The Thesis

*Pussyphus models attention that isn't organized around the self, inside a capture architecture.*

The cat doesn't need the mall to mean something. The escalator doesn't go anywhere. The game-space only exists while you're in it. The complexity — the thing that makes it worth being in — isn't designed. It emerges from nested simple systems interacting, the way a corkscrew emerges from a circle inside a circle that isn't quite a circle.

## Schrödinger's Inversion

The mall is dead but the cat is alive. Schrödinger put the cat in the box and asked about the cat. He never thought to ask about the box.

The box is dead. The cat is alive. He got it exactly backward.

## "The mall is dead but the cat is alive" — 20 Takes

**The container outlived its purpose.** Every vessel eventually learns it was never the point.

**Desire left, presence stayed.** The stores closed one by one and the escalator kept running and the cat kept climbing, because machinery and animals share one trait: neither knows when the reason is gone.

**Architecture as fossil record.** A dead mall is a skeleton of wanting. The cat walks its bones the way water fills a canyon — without nostalgia, without reverence, completely.

**The last moving thing wins.** Everything in the mall stopped except the escalator and the cat. One runs on electricity. The other runs on something we can't turn off.

**Haunting requires no ghosts.** The cat is not haunting the mall. The mall is haunting the cat. She just doesn't notice because she's busy.

**Ruin is just space reopened.** When the stores died the corridors became hallways again. The cat preferred them this way. She always knew they were hallways.

**Emptiness is not nothing.** The mall is empty the way a drum is empty. The cat's paws on the diamond plate prove it. Emptiness has acoustics.

**Purpose decays, motion doesn't.** The escalator was built to deliver shoppers. Now it delivers a cat to nowhere. It has never worked better.

**Flow outlasts meaning.** The meaning left. The cat didn't notice. Flow doesn't require meaning. Meaning requires flow. One of them got the dependency backward.

**Straight lines die first.** The grid failed. The sightlines lead nowhere. The anchor stores are dark. The only thing still navigating is the thing that never moved in straight lines.

**The wiggle survives the grid.** Malls are grids. Grids are impositions. Impositions expire. The cat is a curve. Curves don't expire. They just change what they're moving through.

**Measurement outlives the measured.** The cat is still mapping a building that no longer maps to anything. Attention without an object isn't empty. It's free.

**The audience left first.** Nobody is watching the cat. This is the condition under which the cat does her best work.

**Indifference as preservation.** The mall died of irrelevance. The cat is immune to irrelevance because she never assigned relevance in the first place.

**The wrong direction is correct.** The escalator goes down. The mall went down. The economy went down. The cat goes up. She has no reason. She has legs.

**Nothing for sale, everything available.** The registers are off. The displays are dark. The cat has access to the entire building for the first time. Ownership ended. Occupation began.

**Purgatory has good bones.** The mall is between lives. Not dead, not alive. The cat doesn't mind liminal space. She was born in one. All cats were.

**Obsolescence is a habitat.** The food court is empty. The fountain is dry. The escalator hums. The cat has never lived anywhere better. She requires infrastructure, not commerce.

**The last consumer is consuming nothing.** The cat moves through the mall the way the mall always wanted people to move through it — endlessly, without buying anything. The mall finally got its wish. It just didn't survive it.

**Sisyphus smiled, the boulder didn't.** Camus said we must imagine Sisyphus happy. He didn't say anything about the hill. The escalator doesn't care if the cat is happy. The cat doesn't care if the escalator is a hill. Everyone got what they wanted. Nobody asked.

## Voice Samples

### T.S. Eliot meets Alan Watts

- We shall not cease from ascending, and the end of all our ascending will be to arrive where we started and know the escalator for the first time — but the cat knew it already, which is why she never looked up.

### Carl Sagan meets David Foster Wallace

- Every atom in the cat's body was forged in the interior of a collapsing star, which means the universe spent fourteen billion years arranging itself into something that could ride an escalator sideways and not care, which is either the most profound thing that has ever happened or the least, and the cat — this is the important part — does not experience the distinction.
- Consider that the visible universe contains approximately two trillion galaxies and that not a single one of them has a Cinnabon, and then consider that this mall has two, and ask yourself where the real density problem is.
- The cat has achieved what every contemplative tradition in human history has strained toward, which is the ability to be fully present in a moment without needing that moment to mean anything, and she's done it on a staircase that moves, in a building devoted to want, surrounded by people who've forgotten what they came in for — and the cruel and maybe also beautiful part is that if you described this to the cat she would look at you the way she looks at everything, which is with complete attention and absolutely no interest in your interpretation.

### Mitch Hedberg

- I made a game about a cat going up a down escalator. The escalator disagrees. The cat doesn't care. It's a two-player game but only one of them knows.
- I wanted to make a game with no ending, but I also didn't want to finish making it. So we're both committed.
- You can't die in the game. You can't win either. You can stop playing, but the escalator doesn't know that.
- I made the escalator go down so the cat has to go up. Someone said that's a metaphor. The cat said nothing. She was busy.

### Emil Cioran

- The escalator descends. The cat climbs. This is not courage. Courage would require the belief that the top exists. The cat has no such illusion. She climbs because the alternative is to be carried, and to be carried is to have already agreed to something she was never asked about.
- Every religion has promised that the suffering leads somewhere. The escalator makes no such promise. It is the only honest institution the cat has ever entered, which is why she stays.
- The game has no ending because endings are a courtesy extended by systems that care whether you are paying attention. The escalator does not care. The cat does not care. Only the player cares, which is why only the player suffers.
- To exist is to go nowhere in the wrong direction. The cat merely makes it visible.

## Bo

Bo is a Cornish Rex. Female. Always curious, always exploring, always social. Her fur grows in waves — the breed is a wiggle at the material level.

She howls in alien registers at places she's already decided about. She goes silent in new spaces. Silence is the survey. The howl is the verdict after the fact.

At the building with the international orange stairs, she entered, went quiet, cased the perimeter, climbed the coldest stairs in the building because curiosity outranked thermal comfort, stood on a rail at the threshold between the occupied floors and the empty ones, looked down, came back down, joined the people on the couches, and greeted a visitor from Shanghai.

She approved the building from the couch. You could tell because she stayed.
